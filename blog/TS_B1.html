First Definition of a Translation Surface

We begin by defining the primary object studied in this paper, a translation surface.

Translation Surface as a Complex Surface with Singularities

Let $X$ be a closed, topological surface and let $\Sigma = \{ p_1, p_2, \cdots, p_n \}$ be a finite collection of points on the surface such that

  • $X \setminus \Sigma$ has a translation structure, i.e. it has an atlas of charts into $\mathbb{C}$ such that the transition maps are translations.
  • In a neighborhood of each $p_i \in \Sigma$, there is a chart into $2(m_i + 1)$ half-disks glued as pictured below. Here, we are allowing cone angles at the singularity, where the angle around the point $p_i$ is $2\pi(m_i + 1)$ for $m_i$ a positive integer.

We call the points $p_i$ singularities. Since $X\setminus \Sigma$ has a complex structure, it is orientable. Additionally, the cone angles at the singularities are multiples of $2\pi$, so the holonomy around these points is trivial. Thus, we can make a global choice of "north" on the surface away from $\Sigma$. By default, we typically choose the direction associated with the positive imaginary axis in the charts. This surface, equipped the the charts described and a direction indicating north, is called a translation surface.

There is a canonical choice of metric that comes along with this definition: in each chart, we can pull back the (flat) Euclidean metric on $\mathbb{C}$. Since the transition maps are translations, the differential of any transition map is the identity. Thus, we can glue the metric together to get a flat metric on $X\setminus \Sigma$. This means that we can impose a flat structure on a compact Riemann Surface. The catch is that we will end up with singularities (a non-empty set $\Sigma$) where we have accumulation of curvature unless we have a genus 1 surface. This is a consequence of the Gauss-Bonnet theorem [#] ~\cite{LeeRM19}.
